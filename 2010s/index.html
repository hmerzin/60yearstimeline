President Barack Obama and Russian President Dmitri Medvedev signed the New Strategic Arms Reduction Treaty (New START) to replace the expired Strategic Arms Reduction Treaty of 1991 and the Moscow Treaty of 2002. The Treaty’s verification regime combines appropriate elements of the 1991 START Treaty with new elements tailored to the limitations and structure of the new Treaty. Verification measures under the Treaty include on-site inspections and exhibitions, data exchanges and notifications related to strategic offensive arms and facilities covered by the Treaty, and provisions to facilitate the use of national technical means for treaty monitoring. To increase confidence and transparency, the Treaty also provides for an annual exchange of telemetry on an agreed number of ICBM and SLBM launches. The New START Treaty entered into force in 2011.

Today, the CTBTO PrepCom maintains worldwide 157 operational seismic stations, 10 hydroacoustic stations detecting sound waves in the oceans, 49 listening stations for atmospheric infrasound, 67 radionuclide-sampling stations, and 16 radionuclide laboratories. These and other technologies, many of them being highlighted here today, represent the future of monitoring and verification—and in turn our understanding of global nuclear threats.

The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) reached on July 14, 2015, between Iran and China, France, Germany, Russia, the United Kingdom, the United States, and the European Union relied on both high level political negotiations and the verification capacity of the IAEA to ensure that Iran’s nuclear program will be exclusively peaceful. Under the fortified monitoring and verification parameters of the JCPOA, it will be exceedingly difficult for Iran to set up a covert, parallel program, without early detection. The rigorous verification measures in the JCPOA give us ample time to respond if Iran chooses to violate the terms of the agreement. As Secretary Ernie Moniz stated on the one year anniversary of the deal, “In the past year, the IAEA has gained unprecedented access to Iran’s nuclear facilities and supply chain, and the agency can now use modern safeguards technologies in its monitoring and verification efforts, in order to block possible covert pathways to a nuclear weapon.”
