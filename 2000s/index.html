In 2000, Congress established the National Nuclear Security Administration (NNSA) as a semi-autonomous agency within the U.S. Department of Energy, responsible for enhancing national security through the military application of nuclear science and technology.

Sixteen years later, NNSA maintains and enhances the safety, security, and effectiveness of the U.S. nuclear weapons stockpile without nuclear explosive testing; works to reduce global danger from weapons of mass destruction; provides the U.S. Navy with safe and effective nuclear propulsion systems; and responds to nuclear and radiological emergencies in the United States and abroad.

In 2003, the United States Senate gave its advice and consent to the ratification of the Moscow Treaty on Strategic Offensive Reductions that President Bush and Russia’s President Putin signed on May 24, 2002, in Moscow, Russia. The agreement reduced the nuclear arsenals of the United States and Russia to their lowest levels in decades.

In 2009, President Obama’s historic speech in Prague set out the nuclear agenda for his Administration, focused on moving toward nuclear disarmament while ensuring security and stability. While striving for a nuclear weapons-free world, the President underscored that this will remain a distant goal, and that the U.S. nuclear arsenal must remain safe, secure, and effective for as long as nuclear weapons exist. Following the statement of the Prague Agenda, the United States reduced the role of nuclear weapons in our national security strategy while maintaining a credible deterrent, strengthening nonproliferation efforts, and preventing nuclear terrorism.
